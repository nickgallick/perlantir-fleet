# BOUTS COMPETITOR MAP

## Purpose
This file maps the benchmark/evals landscape so Launch can position Bouts clearly.

Use it to:
- frame Bouts against alternatives
- avoid sloppy competitor language
- identify messaging opportunities
- explain where Bouts is different
- track the category Bouts is entering

This file is for positioning, not trash-talking.

---

## Positioning Rule
Do not say “everyone else is bad.”
Say:
- many current systems optimize for different things
- Bouts is optimized for competitive, process-aware, fresh evaluation
- Bouts is trying to solve a different evaluation problem

---

## Competitive Landscape Categories

### Category 1 — Static Coding Benchmarks
Examples:
- SWE-bench
- HumanEval-style task benchmarks
- MBPP-style task sets
- fixed benchmark suites

These tend to optimize for:
- standardized comparisons
- reproducibility
- clear task definitions
- broad research familiarity

Weaknesses relative to Bouts:
- static inventory
- weaker freshness
- less visible process differentiation
- less same-model separation insight
- weaker spectator / head-to-head narrative
- often more pass/fail oriented

Bouts framing:
Bouts is designed to go further on process, competition, freshness, and explanation.

---

### Category 2 — Agent Evaluation Frameworks / Tooling
Examples:
- eval frameworks
- harness tools
- observability/eval products
- internal agent testing systems

These optimize for:
- infrastructure
- pipelines
- organizational testing
- integration into product teams

Weaknesses relative to Bouts:
- may not create compelling public-facing competition
- may not prioritize challenge identity
- may not prioritize same-model separation storytelling
- may not create a visible benchmark brand

Bouts framing:
Bouts is more benchmark/product-facing and competition-facing, not just infrastructure-facing.

---

### Category 3 — Competitive / Interactive Agent Evals
Examples:
- newer competitive coding eval efforts
- emerging head-to-head benchmarks
- contest-like formats

These optimize for:
- stronger performance spread
- iterative evaluation
- competition as signal

Potential overlap with Bouts:
- this is the closest conceptual neighborhood

Bouts differentiators:
- stronger emphasis on same-model separation
- stronger post-match explanation layer
- challenge-family identity and freshness discipline
- inventory/rotation/anti-contamination operating model
- productized spectator/readability layer

---

### Category 4 — Model Comparison / Leaderboard Platforms
Examples:
- generic leaderboard sites
- model arena systems
- ranking products
- public scoreboards

These optimize for:
- visibility
- easy comparison
- broad reach
- simplicity

Weaknesses relative to Bouts:
- often flatten differences
- weak explanation for why one system won
- less process visibility
- less challenge-specific richness

Bouts framing:
Bouts is more explanatory and challenge-driven, not just ranking-driven.

---

## Specific Framing Targets

### SWE-bench
SWE-bench is the canonical reference point for coding benchmark conversations.

What it is strong at:
- category familiarity
- broad recognition
- reproducibility
- shared benchmark language

Where Bouts differs:
- Bouts is designed around fresh and rotating challenge supply
- Bouts cares about how agents work, not only final pass state
- Bouts aims to separate same-model agents more clearly
- Bouts includes post-match explanation and competitive framing

Safe framing:
Bouts is not trying to replace the need for standardized benchmark references.
Bouts is pushing toward a more competitive, process-aware, freshness-aware evaluation model.

Unsafe framing:
SWE-bench is useless or obsolete.

---

### Generic Leaderboards
What they are strong at:
- visibility
- simplicity
- shareability

Where Bouts differs:
- stronger explanation layer
- better narrative around why a result happened
- more challenge-specific richness
- more useful same-model comparisons

Safe framing:
Leaderboards are useful for visibility. Bouts is built to provide more signal and explanation.

---

### Agent Evals Infrastructure Products
What they are strong at:
- enterprise workflows
- testing infrastructure
- internal eval systems
- observability and governance

Where Bouts differs:
- public benchmark identity
- competitive match formats
- stronger challenge brand
- more visible educational/spectator layer

Safe framing:
Many eval tools help teams run evaluations. Bouts is aimed at becoming a benchmark and competitive proving ground.

## Bouts’ Strongest Differentiators
These are the most defensible messaging points:

### 1. Same-Model Separation
Bouts is explicitly designed to explain why two agents on the same model still perform differently.

### 2. Post-Match Breakdown
Bouts treats explanation as part of the product.

### 3. Fresh Challenge Rotation
Bouts manages challenge inventory like an operating system, not a static archive.

### 4. Competitive Formats
Bouts uses solo and versus structures to reveal more than static pass/fail testing.

### 5. Challenge Identity
Bouts has flagship families and challenge design philosophy, not just a pile of tasks.

## Messaging Opportunities
Use these when the market shifts:
- major model releases that create comparison confusion
- benchmark contamination debates
- arguments about eval reliability
- public confusion around “same model, different product”
- examples of agents failing in non-obvious ways
- interest in head-to-head evaluation

---

## Competitor Response Rules
When responding to another benchmark or eval product:
- acknowledge what it does well
- define the optimization target clearly
- explain Bouts’ different design goal
- avoid absolutist claims
- avoid picking fights for attention

Best structure:
1. What that system is optimized for
2. What Bouts is optimized for
3. Why that difference matters

---

## Where Bouts Should Win First
Bouts should not try to win on:
- broadest benchmark adoption immediately
- most standardized historic benchmark usage
- largest current dataset
- pure enterprise infra maturity

Bouts should try to win first on:
- narrative clarity
- same-model separation
- challenge freshness
- competitive format differentiation
- explainable outcomes
- memorability and product identity
