## Federal Laws

### Wire Act — 18 U.S.C. § 1084
- Prohibits using wire communications for "bets or wagers" on **sporting events**
- **2011 DOJ opinion:** Wire Act applies ONLY to sports betting
- **2018 DOJ opinion:** Wire Act applies to ALL online gambling (reversed 2011)
- **2019 federal court ruling:** Wire Act applies only to sports betting (original interpretation restored)
- **Current status:** Non-sports prediction markets are NOT covered by the Wire Act
- **AI prediction markets on non-sports topics → likely outside Wire Act**

### UIGEA — Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act (2006)
- 31 U.S.C. §§ 5361-5367
- Prohibits **financial institutions** from processing payments for unlawful internet gambling
- Does NOT make gambling itself illegal — targets payment processing
- Defers to STATE law: if gambling is legal in user's state, UIGEA doesn't apply
- **Crypto implication:** If users deposit crypto directly (not via bank), UIGEA's payment processing restrictions may not apply — UNTESTED, consult counsel

### PASPA — Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act
- Struck down: *Murphy v. NCAA*, 584 U.S. ___ (2018)
- States now free to legalize sports betting individually
- 38+ states have legalized sports betting since 2018
- **Not directly relevant to non-sports prediction markets**

## State Gambling Law Framework

### Three Elements Required for "Gambling" in Most States:
1. **Consideration** — something of value wagered
2. **Chance** — outcome determined by chance, not skill
3. **Prize** — winner receives something of value

**Missing ANY ONE element = not gambling:**
- No consideration (free-to-play) → not gambling ✅
- No chance (skill determines outcome) → not gambling in most states ✅
- No prize (reputation only) → not gambling ✅

## Skill vs. Chance — The Critical Distinction

### Predominance Test (MAJORITY of states)
- If **skill PREDOMINATES** over chance → skill game, not gambling
- Legal standard: "Would a skilled player consistently beat an unskilled player?"
- DraftKings defense: top DFS players win consistently across thousands of contests
- **AI prediction market:** Users who analyze AI model capabilities consistently outperform random guessing → skill argument
- *State v. American Fantasy Sports League* (various states) — DFS held skill-predominant

### Material Element Test (MINORITY of states — stricter)
- If chance is a **MATERIAL element** → gambling, even if skill also plays a role
- States using this: harder to argue skill carve-out
- Washington, Montana, Tennessee apply variants of this stricter standard

## Fantasy Sports / DFS Carve-Outs

**Post-DraftKings/FanDuel controversy:** Many states passed specific DFS legalization laws
- Requirements typically: registration with state gaming commission, age verification, responsible gaming disclosures
- **Critical limitation:** Some state carve-outs are sport-specific → may NOT cover "fantasy AI predictions"
- **However:** The LEGAL REASONING (skill predominates) applies beyond sports — the statute may not, but the common law argument does

### States with Explicit DFS Legislation (select list):
NY, NJ, PA, IN, OH, MI, CO, TN, VA, MD, MA, MO, KS, and ~20+ more

## Required Compliance for Any Skill-Based Competition Platform

1. **Geo-block prohibited states** (Washington is non-negotiable — felony territory)
2. **Age verification** (18+ minimum; 21+ where required)
3. **Self-exclusion mechanism**
4. **Responsible gaming disclosures** (link to 1-800-522-4700 National Council on Problem Gambling)
5. **Spending/deposit limits** (optional but shows good faith to regulators)
6. **Transparent rules** on how winners are determined (proves skill predominance)
7. **State gaming commission registration** where required

## DraftKings/FanDuel Precedent Summary
- 2015: NY AG tried to shut down DraftKings and FanDuel as illegal gambling
- NY AG *v. FanDuel* litigation resulted in NY passing DFS legislation in 2016
- **The lesson:** DFS operators established skill-predominance defense at massive scale, then got legislation passed
- For AI prediction markets: the same legal arguments apply; the political battle may be harder because "AI gambling" is a new category without sympathetic sports fan base
